<ul>
<li><b>Hinged dissections of polyominos and polyforms</b>.<br>
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein,
<a href="https://www.cs.purdue.edu/people/gnf/">G. Frederickson</a>,
and
<a href="https://www.stetson.edu/~efriedma/index.html">E. Friedman</a>.<br>
<a href="https://arXiv.org/abs/cs.CG/9907018">arXiv:cs.CG/9907018</a>.<br>
<a href="https://www.cs.ubc.ca/conferences/CCCG/elec_proc/fp37.pdf"><i>11th
Canad. Conf. Comp. Geom.,</i> 1999</a>.<br>
<i>Computational Geometry: Theory and Applications</i> 31 (3): 237&ndash;262, 2005
(special issue for 11th CCCG).
<p>
We show that, for any n, there exists a mechanism formed by connecting
polygons with hinges that can be folded into all possible n-ominos.
Similar results hold as well for n-iamonds, n-hexes, and n-abolos.
<p>
<li><b>Phutball endgames are hard</b>.<br>
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
and D. Eppstein.<br>
<a href="https://arXiv.org/abs/cs.CC/0008025">arXiv:cs.CC/0008025</a>.<br>
<a href="https://www.msri.org/publications/books/Book42/files/dephut.pdf">
<em>More Games of No Chance</em>,
R. J. Nowakowski, ed., MSRI Publications 42, pp. 351&ndash;360</a>.
<p>
We show that, in John Conway's board game Phutball (or Philosopher's
Football), it is NP-complete to determine whether the
current player has a move that immediately wins the game. In
contrast, the similar problems of determining whether there is an
immediately winning move in checkers, or a move that kings a man,
are both solvable in polynomial time. 
<p>
(<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/papers/Phutball/">Erik's
publications page</a>)
<p>
<li><b>Flat foldings of plane graphs with prescribed angles and edge lengths</b>.<br>
<a href="https://zacharyabel.com/">Z. Abel</a>,
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein,
<a href="https://cs.uwaterloo.ca/~alubiw/Site/Anna_Lubiw.html">A. Lubiw</a>, and
<a href="https://www.jaist.ac.jp/~uehara/index-e.html">R. Uehara</a>.<br>
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1408.6771">arXiv:1408.6771</a>.<br>
<i>22nd Int. Symp. Graph Drawing</i>, W&uuml;rzburg, Germany, 2014.<br>
<a href="https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-662-45803-7_23">Springer, <i>Lecture
Notes in Comp. Sci.</i> 8871, 2014, pp. 272&ndash;283.</a><br>
<a href="https://doi.org/10.20382/jocg.v9i1a3"><i>J. Computational
Geometry</i> 9 (1): 74&ndash;93, 2018</a>.
<p>
Given a plane graph with fixed edge lengths, and an assignment of the
angles 0, 180, and 360 to the angles between adjacent edges, we show how
to test whether the angle assignment can be realized by an embedding of
the graph as a flat folding on a line. As a consequence, we can
determine whether two-dimensional cell complexes with one vertex can be
flattened. The main idea behind the result is to show that each face of
the graph can be folded independently of the other faces.
<p>
<li><b>Folding polyominoes into (poly)cubes</b>.<br>
<a href="https://www.ist.tugraz.at/aichholzer/">O. Aichholzer</a>,
<a href="https://www.swarthmore.edu/profile/michael-biro">M. Biro</a>,
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein,
<a href="https://www.ibr.cs.tu-bs.de/users/fekete/?lang=en">S. P. Fekete</a>,
<a href="https://math.mit.edu/directory/profile.php?pid=1468">A. Hesterberg</a>,
<a href="https://www.win.tue.nl/~ikostits/">I. Kostitsyna</a>, and
<a href="https://www.cs.huji.ac.il/~cschmidt/">C. Schmidt</a>.<br>
<a href="https://www.cccg.ca/proceedings/2015/37.pdf"><i>27th Canadian Conference on Computational Geometry</i>, Kingston, Ontario,
Canada, 2015, pp.&nbsp;101&ndash;106</a>.<br>
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.09317">arXiv:1712.09317</a>.<br>
<a href="https://doi.org/10.1142/S0218195918500048"><i>Int. J. Comp. Geom. &amp;
Appl.</i> 28 (3): 197&ndash;226, 2018</a>.
<p>
We classify the polyominoes that can be folded to form the surface of a
cube or polycube, in multiple different folding models that incorporate
the type of fold (mountain or valley), the location of a fold (edges of
the polycube only, or elsewhere such as along diagonals), and whether
the folded polyomino is allowed to pass through the interior of the
polycube or must stay on its surface.
<p>
<li><b>Some polycubes have no edge-unzipping</b>.<br>
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein, and
<a href="https://cs.smith.edu/~orourke/">J. O'Rourke</a>.<br>
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.08433">arXiv:1907.08433</a>.<br>
<i><a href="http://cccg.ca/proceedings/2020/proceedings.pdf">Proc. 32nd Canadian Conference on Computational Geometry</a></i>, 2020, pp. 101&ndash;105.
<p>
We find polycubes that cannot be cut along a simple path through their
vertices and edges and unfolded to form a flat polygon in the plane.
<p>
<li><b>Existence and hardness of conveyor belts</b>.<br>
<a href="https://amath.washington.edu/people/molly-baird">M. Baird</a>,
<a href="https://sites.math.washington.edu/~billey/">S. C. Billey</a>,
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein,
<a href="https://www.ibr.cs.tu-bs.de/users/fekete/?lang=en">S. P. Fekete</a>,
<a href="https://math.washington.edu/people/graham-w-gordon">G. Gordon</a>,
<a href="https://sites.math.washington.edu/~stgriff/">S. Griffin</a>,
<a href="http://www.ams.sunysb.edu/~jsbm/">J. S. B. Mitchell</a>, and
<a href="http://www.math.ucsd.edu/~jswanson/">J. P. Swanson</a>.<br>
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.07668">arXiv:1908.07668</a>.<br>
<a href="https://doi.org/10.37236/9782"><i>Electronic
J. Combinatorics</i> 27 (4), Paper P4.25, 2020</a>.
<p>
A conveyor belt is a tight simple closed curve that touches each of a
given set of disjoint disks. We show that certain special families of
disks always have a conveyor belt, but that it is NP-complete to tell
whether one exists for arbitrary families of disks. It is always
possible to add a linear number of "guide disks" to a given set of disks
to allow them to be connected by a conveyor belt.
<p>
<li><b>Acutely triangulated, stacked, and very ununfoldable polyhedra</b>.<br>
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>, and
D. Eppstein.<br>
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.14525">arXiv:2007.14525</a>.<br>
<i><a href="http://cccg.ca/proceedings/2020/proceedings.pdf">Proc. 32nd Canadian Conference on Computational Geometry</a></i>, 2020, pp. 106&ndash;113.
<p>
We construct non-convex but topologically spherical polyhedra in which
all faces are acute triangles, with no unfolded net.
<p>
<li><b>New results in sona drawing: hardness and TSP separation</b>.<br>
<a href="https://chiumk.github.io/">M.-W. Chiu</a>,
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein,
<a href="https://groups.csail.mit.edu/mac/users/bob/">R. Hearn</a>,
<a href="https://math.mit.edu/directory/profile.php?pid=1468">A. Hesterberg</a>,
<a href="https://engineering.tufts.edu/people/faculty/matias-korman">M. Korman</a>,
<a href="https://www.ist.tugraz.at/parada/">I. Parada</a>, and
M. Rudoy.<br>
<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.15784">arXiv:2007.15784</a>.<br>
<i><a href="http://cccg.ca/proceedings/2020/proceedings.pdf">Proc. 32nd Canadian Conference on Computational Geometry</a></i>, 2020, pp. 63&ndash;72.
<p>
A sona drawing is a self-crossing curve in the plane that separates each
of a given system of points into its own region. We show that it is hard
to find the shortest such curve, and we explore how much shorter than
the TSP it can be.
<p>
<li><b>Multifold tiles of polyominoes and convex lattice polygons</b>.<br>
K. Chida,
<a href="https://erikdemaine.org/">E. Demaine</a>,
<a href="https://martindemaine.org/">M. Demaine</a>,
D. Eppstein,
<a href="https://math.mit.edu/directory/profile.php?pid=1468">A. Hesterberg</a>,
T. Horiyama,
<a href="https://di.ulb.ac.be/algo/iacono/">J. Iacono</a>,
<a href="https://www.alg.cei.uec.ac.jp/itohiro/">H. Ito</a>,
<a href="http://cgm.cs.mcgill.ca/~sl/">S. Langerman</a>,
<a href="https://www.jaist.ac.jp/~uehara/index-e.html">R. Uehara</a>, and
<a href="https://jazz.cias.osakafu-u.ac.jp/~uno/index-e.htm">Y. Uno</a>.<br>
<a href="http://www.math.science.cmu.ac.th/tjcdcggg/Book-abstract.pdf"><i>23rd Thailand-Japan Conference on Discrete and Computational
Geometry, Graphs, and Games</i>, 2021, pp. 28&ndash;29</a>.<br>
<p>
We investigate shapes whose congruent copies can cover the plane
exactly \(k\) times per point, but not a number of times that is a
nonzero integer
smaller than \(k\). We find polyominoes with this property for all \(k\ge 2\), and convex integer-coordinate polygons with this property for \(k=5\)
and for all \(k\ge 7\).
<p>
